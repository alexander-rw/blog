# A decade of engineering

I've been writing software in some form for over a decade. I've written software for fun, and I've had professional roles at a few different companies. 
This blog is the culmination of my thoughts about my career so far, and the tech leadership experience that I've built up over the last decade.

## My first professional opportunity

I started programming back in school; A friend of mine introduced me to MS Access and VBA and we spend time during our IT classes working out what we could do with technology (and causing mischief). 
It wasn't until I was a few years older, though, that I really started enjoying writing code. I tinkered with things I owned, tried (and mostly failed) to learn java when attempting to build mods in minecraft.

At the age of 17, I had an opportunity to do a 6 week paid internship at Barclays Bank (as part of a company called Career Academies, now [Career Ready](https://careerready.org.uk/)). Two things were abundantly clear to me:

1. I enjoyed programming in a professional setting
2. A lot of people that were software engineers didn't do computer science at degree level, so I'd probably be ok not doing the same

At that point, I was already fairly deep into applying for physics undergraduate degrees, so having a career path for after university that wasn't physics research was a nice option. I ended up not enjoying my degree, for numerous reasons, 
but it did prepare me for something common in software and more senior professional roles: ambiguous problem solving is part of the game, and (in my opinion) is the fun bit.

After that, I went to university and eventually landed my first real software job: Software Engineer @ [Tessella](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tessella).
They were (are) a consultancy that developed solutions for complex domains, with many clients in the life sciences, chemicals, and other industries where knowledge about the domain that you worked in was just as important as the software that you built and maintained.

I spent most of my time at Tessella working on projects for AkzoNobel. Readers of this blog may know AkzoNobel as the company that own Dulux, but may not know that there's a whole other world of 
high performance paint coatings for other applications than the walls of your kitchen or living room. I worked on a few projects at Tessella that focused on the coatings for ships, and anything related to that - including, 
how to chart an optimal course for a boat and then re-paint the ship's hull in dry-dock. For a large container vessel, the (relatively) small cost of a strip and re-paint, with a high quality low drag coating, of the entire hull that sits around the waterline, 
would run in the region of tens or hundreds of thousands of pounds (or more). [It would also save many times that in fuel costs](https://www.international-marine.com/en/news/akzonobel-and-tesella-win-leading-it-award) over the ship's lifetime, and reduce carbon emissions along the way - a win/win for both the company and the climate.

I found this work really interesting, and I learned a lot from my peers and my manager. The patience that they showed me is something that I've tried to take with me into other jobs, and something I try to emulate.

## The next few years

After Tessella, I worked at a couple of other places: Altitude Angel, a company that focused on integrating drones into the global aviation airspace, and [Zing Dev](https://www.zing.dev/), a pure-play twilio consultancy. 

Moving between these three companies taught me something important about the sort of work that _I_ like to do: I find it significantly more enjoyable doing product work compared to consultancy-style work. 
The two aren't fundamentally different, but the balance of work is. I found that at both Tessella (a large consultancy), and Zing (a much smaller consultancy), the same was true:
you traded off thinking about and impacting strategic work with clients, for direct ownership and experience managing, improving, and running the software you built. 
I found it much more rewarding to work on a product (or set of related products).

By the time I left Zing, I was leading projects for our client portfolio that, role wise, was something between technical lead and TPM. 
I had to think about overrunning budgets, delivery of quality vs speed, and managing clients, with IC in there too. I didn't write as much code at that job as I would have liked, but I reviewed a hell of a lot more than previous jobs. The balance of what I did shifted, and I ended up doing a lot of product and strategy work with clients that was really rewarding.

Overall, I liked this sort of work, and wanted to do more - at a larger company with more engineers, and therefore more surface area to influence.

## Tech Leading a Data Platform Team

In november 2023, I joined Liberis' data platform team.

I'd never worked in data or analytics engineering before. Liberis at the time was a rapidly growing tech company, and their data stack was fine for the time, but wouldn't get them to the next phase of what the company needed.
They had a central reporting team, who weren't a part of the engineering function - just by nature of the way the company was set up years prior. A newer treatment of data needed newer a data platform, and the engineering and company wide strategy that went along with it.

My engineering manager at the time decided on the strategy behind this, and we set to work - designing a data platform from the ground up, with more mordern engineering practices. We decided on [dlt](https://dlthub.com/) for data load, [dbt](https://www.getdbt.com/) for transformation, and [Looker](https://cloud.google.com/looker) for analytics/visualisation. 
We aimed to make the platform as self-serve as possible, but in reality this meant playing a hybrid of product team (for our internal users) and platform team, so that we could deliver value (by providing data engineering or analytics capabilities in ways that the business didn't have before), or rebuilding data products while teaching the users or consumers of those datasets how to contribute to these new data products.

In december 2024, our engineering manager went on maternity leave and I formally took over the management of the team for the year. 
This was hard; it took me a couple of months to re-balance and manage the change in workload, from much more IC work to management, with more strategic, longer term thinking and stakeholder management on a team that was underresourced for the amount of work it needed to do. 
I found that blocking time and guarding my hours was a key to this - as well as declining work that didn't provide as much value as the most important thing that you're expected to be working on.

In June, Liberis went full on into delivery mode to launch a new ambitious product, and needed to be able to answer questions of the commercial performance of our product. I'd been tasked with leading a cross functional project that did this; work ranged from backend contributions for our new services to be able to handle established and rapidly evolving data models, to data and analytics engineering to model the data we were getting into our systems into something that was useful for commercial and product teams to be able to bite at and answer questions.

## Raising the ceiling, not the floor

In late july 2024, I went to get a coffee with someone in the industry I respect a lot, after meeting at a conference. A single moment **really** stuck out to me in that conversation. 

We were discussing technical leadership, and I was asking for any tips around how to handle large, cross functional projects. I asked this specifically because I'd led these before at Liberis, was leading one again, and found that they went ok, but not _great_.

I was given a simple but very impactful piece of advice: Identify the person that's most capable of replacing you, and level them up so that you can move onto more impactful things.
This is a requirement (IMO) for staff+ level engineers, and is also where I get a lot of my energy - teaching someone to be able to do something without you might not be "great" for job security, but the feeling of watching someone you've mentored be able to take what you have taught them and apply that learning is unparalleled.
Crucially this isn't just a way of freeing up your time for other things - it's also a way of giving the engineers that you manage or work with growth opportunities, and you're then able to take that away to future situations that could arise by saying "Hey, this thing is coming up, and I think that this person would be good to contribute or lead - because i've seen them do it before".

The whole idea of raising the ceiling of a team, not the floor, was implicit to me. I often tried to do this as a lead at the same time as raising the floor. 
Having it spelled out explicitly made me rethink the way that I approached mentoring and working with the cross-functional team I was working with at the time. That project's goals were pretty clear - build out our analytics capabilities within our new product in a way that would make it easy for product engineering teams to work with their analytical users without needing a platform team in the loop at all. We got very close to that at the end of the project (~November 2025), but that wasn't the thing that I focused on for most of that project. 

## Pair programming (and sharing context)

Part of the cross functional team that I worked with included commercial analysts. They were representing our internal commercial users - who were the first users of the analytics that we were building - but also were themselves heavy users of our platform. 

Being this close to your users is great. It's a lightning fast way of [shortening the feedback loop](https://alexander-rw.com/blog/shortening-the-feedback-loop), so I decided to leverage what I personally think is the best way of knowledge transfer and upskilling: pair programming. 

My thoughts about this stem from what I mentioned above - shortening a feedback loop is so impactful and I've found that this is the best way to do it. Our analysts were both technically capable, and knowledgeable in their domains - they just didn't know the ins and outs of using our specific platform that we'd built, and 
didn't know all the tips and tricks that an engineer would pick up living in the tooling and workflows that we were in day to day. 

Within a month, the two analysts who were part of the cross-functional team I was working in were significantly more comfortable with our tooling (having used sql before, but never contributing code to a repository or using the terminal in the same way that engineers are used to). Within two, they were contributing PRs in small and fast increments and I was blown away. 
By the end of that project, they were shifted far enough left and had been bought into our engineering practices enough that I was very happy stepping away from that project and letting them own what they'd built out, with absolutely no worries in my mind.

I could have taken the opposite (maybe better described as commercially/product minded) approach here. We would maybe have delivered more, but only in the short term, because we would have then had to hand responsibility for owning the models, dashboards, and other assets to the product teams and analysts with significantly less context. The problem here is that this doesn't pay off in the long run, and is really where I think some organisations over index: 
short term gains are fine, but you need to be strategic and really careful about when to trade off that short term win, knowing that it will slow you down in the long run (in some cases). It should be a deliberate choice, not a default one.

## Getting out of the way

I decided near the end of 2025 to move on from Liberis. Leaving a role, for me, is always a bittersweet feeling, marred with some level of risk and emotional apprehension. You're not always sure if leaving is the right thing. Still, I've never regretted leaving a role I worked at - at some point, outgrowing your role and wanting to leave because of it is ok. 
Growth for me is pushing myself out of my comfort zone, and then being brutally honest with myself about what I have and haven't done well at.

## Conclusion

Getting out of the way doesn't always mean leaving - it can also mean moving internally so that you can let someone step into the role that you were doing, or can even mean staying where you are but slowly peeling away certain aspects of leadership and delegating that responsibility
to your team, so that they are able to take on, prioritise, and deliver without as much input from you.

To me, being a leader means building something that keeps going without you. Whether that means [stepping back and zooming out](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rXgzxz4xiJY), leaving entirely, or just shutting your laptop screen and letting someone else drive. That's the part i've found the hardest - and the most worth doing.
